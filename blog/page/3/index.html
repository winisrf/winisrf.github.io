Hyperparameter Tuning Analysis for Food-11 Image Classification

Posted on 2026-01-01 Edited on 2026-07-19 In Archive

Abstract. This report investigates the impact of several important hyperparameters and regularization methods on the image classification performance of deep neural networks. Experiments were conducted on the Food-11 dataset using the EfficientNet-B0 architecture. The study consists of four parts: learning rate comparison, learning rate schedule comparison, weight decay comparison, and mixup data augmentation. The results show that an initial learning rate of 0.025 provides the best balance between convergence speed and stability, constant learning rate outperforms cosine annealing in validation performance under the current setting, stronger weight decay improves generalization, and mixup provides a slight improvement in validation and test performance at the cost of lower training accuracy.

The experimental results show that the learning rate of 0.025 significantly outperformed the other two groups. This demonstrates that when the learning rate is too large, the parameters are updated more rapidly at each step, making it more prone to oscillations during optimization and hindering the model from converging. The learning rate of 0.001 showed the worst performance in both training and validation, as its learning process was too slow to learn enough effective features within the current training budget.

In summary, a learning rate of 0.025 achieved the best balance between convergence speed and training stability. On the one hand, it is not as aggressively updated as 0.1, on the other hand, it drives model optimization faster than 0.001, achieving optimal results on both the training and validation sets. Based on this experimental result, 0.025 can be selected as the initial learning rate for subsequent experiments.

Revisiting FRSKD: Curriculum Augmentation, Mask-Aware Distillation, and Efficient Attention for Self-Knowledge Distillation

Posted on 2026-01-01 Edited on 2026-07-19 In Archive

Abstract

Feature Refinement via Self-Knowledge Distillation (FRSKD) attaches a BiFPN-shaped self-teacher to a classifier network and distills a refined feature map together with a soft label back into the classifier. On top of a faithful reproduction of the CIFAR-100 ResNet-18 benchmark (77.41 ± 0.17% over three seeds, versus the paper’s 77.71 ± 0.14%), we investigate three orthogonal extensions. First, we replace the fixed CutMix policy with an adaptive, curriculum-style schedule that anneals both the mixing strength and the application probability across training; a single-seed run reaches 80.22% Best / 80.05% Last on CIFAR-100, matching the paper’s fixed-CutMix result of 80.49% Best within typical seed variance. Second, we make the self-distillation losses mask-aware by propagating the CutMix mask into both the feature distillation and the soft-label distillation; somewhat unexpectedly, this produces a negligible change over vanilla FRSKD+CutMix, which we attribute to the coarseness of deep feature maps on 32×32 inputs. Third, we study a cheaper classifier by replacing Squeeze-and-Excitation with Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) in EfficientNet-B0 and coupling the change with modern regularization; the result is a 4.8× speedup but an 11% accuracy drop, which we dissect as a resolution/capacity bottleneck rather than an attention-mechanism failure. Together, these experiments suggest that scheduling augmentation strength is a simple and effective knob for self-distillation, while mask-propagation and aggressive architectural pruning require more care than drop-in substitution.

Introduction

Knowledge distillation (KD) has become a standard tool for producing compact classifiers that retain most of the accuracy of a larger teacher network (Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean 2015). A practical limitation of classical KD is that it requires a pretrained teacher, which is expensive both to train and to store. Self-knowledge distillation (self-KD) removes this dependency by letting a network distill from itself, either through data-augmentation consistency (Xu and Liu 2019; Yun et al. 2020; Lee, Hwang, and Shin 2020) or through auxiliary classifier branches (Zhang et al. 2019; Lan, Zhu, and Gong 2018).

Among self-KD methods, FRSKD (Ji et al. 2021) stands out by introducing an auxiliary self-teacher network built from a BiFPN-style top-down and bottom-up feature pyramid (Tan, Pang, and Le 2020). The self-teacher aggregates multi-scale feature maps from the classifier and distills both a refined feature map (via attention transfer (Zagoruyko and Komodakis 2016)) and a soft label back into the classifier. FRSKD reports state-of-the-art self-KD results on CIFAR-100, TinyImageNet, and several fine-grained datasets, and composes well with data augmentations such as MixUp (Zhang et al. 2018) and CutMix (Yun et al. 2019).

Two aspects of FRSKD motivate the present work. First, CutMix is applied with fixed strength and probability throughout training, even though the classifier’s representations are substantially less stable in early epochs than in late epochs. This suggests that a curriculum over augmentation strength could better match the model’s representational maturity. Second, when CutMix is combined with FRSKD, the distillation losses remain unaware of the mask: the attention-transfer term pools each feature map spatially into a single vector, averaging across two semantically distinct regions drawn from two different source images. Whether exposing the mask to the losses helps is an empirical question that, to our knowledge, has not been answered.

Finally, a practical concern when distillation is bolted on top of a heavy classifier backbone is training cost. We therefore also consider EfficientNet-B0 (Tan and Le 2019) as a candidate classifier and study whether replacing its Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) (Hu, Shen, and Sun 2018) blocks with the parameter-free Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) (Wang et al. 2020) can retain accuracy at lower computational cost.

Contributions. We make the following contributions.

  1. Faithful baseline reproduction. We reproduce the CIFAR-100 ResNet-18 FRSKD baseline over three seeds, obtaining 77.41 ± 0.17% (Last) / 77.57 ± 0.17% (Best), within 0.30% of the paper’s reported average and with a matching standard deviation. This gives us a calibrated reference against which to judge the extensions below.
  2. Adaptive CutMix scheduling. We introduce a curriculum over the CutMix Beta parameter αt\alpha_tαt​ and application probability ptp_tpt​, integrated into FRSKD as a new augmentation mode. A single-seed run achieves 80.05% / 80.22% (Last / Best), matching the paper’s fixed FRSKD+CutMix of 80.49% within typical seed variance — consistent with the hypothesis that a weaker early-phase policy does not harm final accuracy.
  3. Mask-aware distillation losses (negative result with analysis). We propose region-split attention transfer and λ\lambdaλ-weighted soft-label KD to make FRSKD losses mask-aware under CutMix. On CIFAR-100 with ResNet-18, this yields essentially no improvement over the vanilla combination (+0.02% Best, +0.16% Last, within noise). We identify the cause: on 32×32 inputs, the deepest feature block has a spatial extent of only 4×4, which limits the information added by mask propagation.
  4. Efficient attention for an alternative backbone (negative result with analysis). Replacing SE with ECA in EfficientNet-B0, combined with label smoothing, MixUp, and stronger weight decay, narrows the train/test generalization gap from 35.33% to 21.31% and accelerates training by 4.8×, but absolute test accuracy drops from 77.42% to 66.24%. We attribute this to a resolution/capacity bottleneck inherent to running EfficientNet-B0 on 32×32 inputs, not to the choice of attention.

Background and Related Work

Knowledge distillation. Classical KD (Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean 2015) trains a student to match the softened logits of a pretrained teacher. Extensions distill intermediate features (Romero et al. 2015; Zagoruyko and Komodakis 2016; Heo et al. 2019), relational structure (Park et al. 2019; Peng et al. 2019), or factorized representations (Kim, Park, and Kwak 2018).

Self-knowledge distillation. Self-KD removes the pretrained teacher. DDGSD (Xu and Liu 2019) and CS-KD (Yun et al. 2020) encourage consistent predictions across augmented or same-class instances. SLA (Lee, Hwang, and Shin 2020) augments labels by coupling the classification task with self-supervised rotation. BYOT (Zhang et al. 2019) and ONE (Lan, Zhu, and Gong 2018) attach auxiliary weak classifiers to intermediate layers and aggregate signals back into the main network. FRSKD (Ji et al. 2021) differs by using an auxiliary stronger self-teacher (a modified BiFPN (Tan, Pang, and Le 2020)) that produces a refined feature map, enabling both feature-map and soft-label distillation.

Data augmentation with mixing. MixUp (Zhang et al. 2018) trains on convex combinations of inputs and labels, while CutMix (Yun et al. 2019) replaces a rectangular region of one image with a patch from another and mixes labels by area. Both have been widely reported to compose with KD. Curriculum-style augmentation schedules have been explored elsewhere (e.g., progressive RandAugment magnitudes (Cubuk et al. 2020)), but curriculum CutMix for self-KD, to our knowledge, has not been isolated.

Channel attention. Squeeze-and-Excitation (Hu, Shen, and Sun 2018) recalibrates channel activations using a two-layer MLP with a reduction bottleneck. ECA (Wang et al. 2020) shows that a lightweight one-dimensional convolution across channels provides comparable benefits with far fewer parameters, and is the drop-in replacement we use later.

Adaptive CutMix for FRSKD

Motivation

FRSKD+CutMix is reported to reach 80.49% Best accuracy on CIFAR-100 (Ji et al. 2021), a 2.8% gain over FRSKD alone. In the official implementation, CutMix is applied with a fixed mixing distribution Beta(α, α) and a fixed application probability throughout training.

This fixed policy is arguably mismatched with the training dynamics of the classifier. Early in training, representations are unstable and the distillation signal from the self-teacher is itself weak — aggressive CutMix at this stage risks adding label noise before the network has learned any discriminative features. Late in training, once representations have settled, stronger augmentation becomes a useful regularizer against overfitting (the baseline reaches ~99.9% training accuracy, indicating ample capacity for absorbing extra regularization). We therefore propose to keep FRSKD’s distillation design unchanged and modulate only the augmentation schedule.

Analysis

We see three plausible reasons for the negative result. (1) Spatial granularity. ResNet-18 on 32×32 CIFAR inputs produces its deepest feature block at 4×4. Taken together, we interpret the result as evidence that mask-awareness, at least at CIFAR resolutions, does not introduce new information beyond what spatial pooling plus mixed labels already provide.

Efficient Attention: ECA-Enhanced EfficientNet-B0

Motivation

Distillation is typically bolted on top of a fixed backbone. A natural question is whether a more parameter-efficient classifier can maintain competitive accuracy at lower training cost. EfficientNet-B0 (Tan and Le 2019) is an attractive candidate, but its default Squeeze-and-Excitation blocks account for a substantial fraction of both its parameters and its wall-clock time. A preliminary experiment on CIFAR-100 showed that naively removing SE brings per-epoch time from 78.6 s to 19.3 s (4×) but collapses generalization: training accuracy stays at 99.79% while test accuracy drops to 64.46%, a 35.33% gap. We therefore ask: does a lightweight attention mechanism, coupled with explicit regularization, recover the generalization benefit that SE was providing while retaining the speed benefit of its removal?

Generalization vs. absolute accuracy. The resolution bottleneck. We argue that this is a resolution/capacity bottleneck rather than an ECA failure. EfficientNet-B0 was designed for ImageNet-scale 224×224 inputs; on 32×32 CIFAR images its first few stages see a receptive field that covers a disproportionate fraction of the image, and its depth is underutilized. SE’s two-layer MLP provides enough global context to partially compensate for the lost spatial extent; ECA’s lightweight 1D convolution cannot. Diagnostic evidence: the train accuracy with ECA + regularization is 87.41%, versus 99.79% for SE-removed and 99.85% for SE-kept — i.e., the model is now underfitting. Regularization successfully prevented overconfident memorization, but it did so on a backbone that is already capacity-limited at 32×32 resolution.

Implications. The decoupling of generalization gap (improved) from absolute accuracy (worsened) is itself informative: it tells us that on CIFAR-100, with the EfficientNet-B0 backbone, SE is not primarily acting as a regularizer (label smoothing and MixUp suffice for that role) but as a representation-quality module. Reproducing its effect with ECA likely requires higher input resolution or deeper channel interaction; simple drop-in substitution is insufficient.

Discussion and Limitations

On the value of negative results. Two of our three extensions fail to improve absolute accuracy in their direct comparison. We report them anyway, because each failure carries a diagnosable cause — coarse deep feature maps on CIFAR resolutions in the mask-aware case, and a resolution/capacity bottleneck in the ECA case. Such diagnoses are more useful than a headline number. They also calibrate expectations for follow-up work: mask-awareness is likely more useful on ImageNet-resolution inputs, and ECA-substitution likely needs larger input resolution to close the gap to SE.

Compute-driven limitations. All experiments ran on a single NVIDIA L4 GPU under Google Colab’s time quotas, and we were unable to re-run or extend configurations once the submission window closed. This forced two compromises that we state plainly so that readers can calibrate our claims. (i) The Adaptive CutMix result is single-seed. We report it as indicative, not definitive, and refrain from claiming a win over the paper’s fixed CutMix. (ii) The mask-aware study used a halved 100-epoch schedule and only the combined both configuration; relative comparisons to the matched-schedule vanilla baseline remain meaningful, but 200-epoch and per-modification numbers would allow a stronger attribution. These limitations do not affect the reproducibility of the reported numbers (the baseline is a full three-seed study, and all code and logs are released), and they inform our choice of framing throughout: we report diagnosed observations rather than headline claims.

Threats to validity. Single-seed numbers at the ~80% accuracy level are noisy (±0.2–0.3% is typical). Wherever compute permitted we reported three-seed statistics and explicitly annotated single-seed results to avoid ambiguity.

Conclusion

We reproduced the CIFAR-100 ResNet-18 FRSKD baseline faithfully (77.41 ± 0.17%, within 0.3% of the paper), and used that reference to evaluate three extensions. Curriculum-style Adaptive CutMix reached 80% with a single seed, supporting the idea that scheduling augmentation strength — not just choosing its distribution — is a useful design axis for self-distillation. Mask-aware distillation losses did not improve over vanilla FRSKD+CutMix, which we attribute to the coarse 4×4 deep feature grid on CIFAR images. ECA with modern regularization on EfficientNet-B0 accelerated training by 4.8× and shrank the generalization gap, but at the cost of 11% absolute accuracy — a cost we diagnose as a resolution/capacity bottleneck inherent to 32×32 inputs. Together, these three experiments give a practitioner-oriented view of which FRSKD extensions are and are not likely to pay off on small-image benchmarks.

How Text Chunking Strategy Affects Retrieval-Augmented Generation Quality

Posted on 2026-01-01 Edited on 2026-07-19 In Archive

Abstract

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) grounds large language model (LLM) answers in external evidence, but the quality of retrieved context depends heavily on how source documents are split into chunks. This design choice is often treated as an afterthought despite its direct impact on what the LLM sees at inference time. We present a controlled experiment that isolates text chunking strategy as the sole independent variable in an end-to-end RAG pipeline. Using 195 question–answer pairs drawn from the MS MARCO passage-ranking dataset and a 688 KB general-knowledge corpus, we compare two LangChain splitters — fixed-size character splitting and recursive character splitting — while holding all other parameters constant: the same embedding model (all-MiniLM-L6-v2), the same vector store (ChromaDB), the same chunk size (512 characters) and overlap (50 characters), the same top-k retrieval (k=5), and the same GPT-based answer generator and judge. The recursive splitter achieves 59.0% accuracy (115/195) versus 53.8% (105/195) for the fixed splitter, a gain of +5.2 percentage points. Average quality scores (1–5 scale) improve from 3.15 to 3.29. Agreement analysis shows the recursive splitter wins 22 questions that the fixed splitter loses, while the reverse occurs for only 12 questions. The quality distribution is strikingly bimodal: answers are either clearly correct (quality 4–5) or clearly wrong (quality 1–2), suggesting that retrieval — not generation — is the primary bottleneck. Our results confirm that preserving paragraph and sentence boundaries during chunking yields more semantically coherent chunks that embed and retrieve more reliably.

1. Introduction

Large language models have demonstrated remarkable text generation capabilities, yet they can produce factually incorrect or outdated information when relying solely on parametric knowledge. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) addresses this limitation by augmenting the LLM’s input with relevant passages retrieved from an external knowledge base, grounding its answers in verifiable evidence.

A typical RAG pipeline involves several stages: the source corpus is split into chunks, each chunk is embedded into a dense vector, the vectors are indexed in a vector store, and at query time the most similar chunks are retrieved and fed to the LLM as context for answer generation. Among these stages, the chunking strategy — how the raw text is divided into segments — is a critical yet often under-examined design decision. Naive fixed-size splitting divides text at arbitrary character boundaries, potentially cutting sentences or paragraphs mid-word. In contrast, recursive character splitting attempts to respect natural text boundaries by first trying to split on paragraph breaks, then sentence breaks, then word breaks, falling back to character-level splitting only when necessary.

In this work, we isolate chunking strategy as the only variable in a complete RAG pipeline. We hold constant the corpus, embedding model, vector store, chunk size, overlap, retrieval depth, answer-generation LLM, and evaluation method. This controlled setup allows us to attribute any performance difference directly to the splitter. We evaluate on 195 MS MARCO questions using GPT-as-judge and find that the recursive splitter achieves a +5.2 percentage-point accuracy improvement over the fixed splitter.

2. Related Work

Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Lewis et al. (2020) introduced the RAG framework, combining a pre-trained sequence-to-sequence model with a neural retriever to generate knowledge-grounded responses. Their work demonstrated that retrieval augmentation significantly improves performance on knowledge-intensive NLP tasks compared to purely parametric approaches.

Text Chunking Strategies. The LangChain framework provides several text splitters for document preprocessing. The CharacterTextSplitter splits text at fixed character intervals regardless of content, while the RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter uses a hierarchy of separators to split at the most semantically meaningful boundary available. Industry best-practice guides from Pinecone and Weaviate emphasize that chunk boundaries significantly affect retrieval quality.

Embedding Models. Reimers and Gurevych (2019) introduced Sentence-BERT, a modification of BERT that produces semantically meaningful sentence embeddings suitable for cosine similarity search. The all-MiniLM-L6-v2 variant provides a strong balance between embedding quality and computational efficiency with 384-dimensional output vectors.

LLM-as-Judge Evaluation. Zheng et al. (2023) demonstrated that strong LLMs can serve as automated evaluators, achieving high agreement with human judgments. We adopt this approach for scalable evaluation of answer quality across our 195-question test set.

MS MARCO. Nguyen et al. (2016) introduced the MS MARCO dataset, a large-scale reading comprehension and question-answering benchmark based on real Bing queries. We use a subset of 195 question–answer pairs as our evaluation set.

3. Approach

3.1 Pipeline Overview

Our RAG pipeline consists of seven stages: (1) Corpus Loading — load all text files using LangChain’s DirectoryLoader; (2) Chunking — split documents using one of two splitter strategies; (3) Embedding — encode each chunk into a 384-dim vector using all-MiniLM-L6-v2; (4) Indexing — store vectors in ChromaDB; (5) Retrieval — retrieve the top-5 most similar chunks per question; (6) Answer Generation — prompt GPT with retrieved chunks as context; (7) Evaluation — GPT-as-judge produces correctness (0/1), quality (1–5), and a free-text justification.

3.2 Experimental Variable: Two Splitters

The only parameter that differs between conditions is the splitter. The fixed-size splitter (CharacterTextSplitter) is configured with separator="", chunk_size=512, and chunk_overlap=50. It divides text purely by character count, ignoring semantic boundaries, and produced 1486 chunks from the corpus.

The recursive splitter (RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter) is configured with the same chunk_size and chunk_overlap, using the default separator hierarchy ["\n\n", "\n", " ", ""]. It first attempts to split on double newlines (paragraph boundaries), then single newlines, then spaces, falling back to character-level splitting only for passages with no whitespace. It produced 2113 chunks from the corpus.

The recursive splitter produces more chunks because it respects natural boundaries rather than forcing exact 512-character segments. Each chunk tends to be a complete paragraph or group of sentences, whereas fixed chunks frequently break mid-sentence.

To understand when and why each splitter succeeds or fails, we examine specific examples from each outcome category.

Inspection suggests the recursive splitter preserves a complete paragraph containing the relevant context, whereas the fixed splitter cuts the passage mid-sentence. Similarly, question q_15 (“what is Tom Cruise doing now”) yields an answer about selling property under the fixed splitter (incorrect) but a correct answer mentioning Rebecca Ferguson under the recursive splitter, because the recursive chunk keeps the complete paragraph intact. Question q_22 (“what is treatment for jaundice”) fails entirely under the fixed splitter (“I cannot answer”), but the recursive splitter retrieves a chunk containing phototherapy information and answers correctly.

Fixed wins, recursive loses (12 cases). Question q_10 (“cost to file bankruptcy in virginia”) is answered correctly by the fixed splitter (quality 4) with the exact figure of $306, but the recursive splitter includes an incorrect Chapter 13 cost and receives quality 3 (incorrect). Question q_18 (“What Does Lop means”) is correctly answered as “Lack of Prosecution” by the fixed splitter (quality 5), but the recursive splitter returns an unrelated dictionary definition (quality 1). In these cases, the fixed splitter’s chunks happen to isolate the answer within a compact passage, while the recursive splitter over-segments or includes misleading context.

Both fail (68 cases). Many involve questions where the corpus likely lacks a closely matching passage. For instance, questions about niche topics (q_17: “what is baseball twine made of”, q_24: “what is a purified phytochemical”) result in “I cannot answer” from both splitters, indicating corpus coverage gaps rather than chunking failures.

Bimodal quality pattern. Across both conditions, quality scores 1–3 correspond almost exclusively to incorrect answers, while 4–5 correspond to correct answers. This clean separation confirms that the LLM either has good context and produces a strong answer, or has poor context and fails entirely. The generation step adds little noise — the bottleneck is retrieval.

6. Conclusion

This study demonstrates that text chunking strategy has a measurable impact on RAG pipeline quality. In a controlled experiment where only the splitter varies, the recursive character splitter achieves 59.0% accuracy compared to 53.8% for the fixed-size splitter, an improvement of +5.2 percentage points. The key mechanism is that the recursive splitter preserves paragraph and sentence boundaries, producing semantically self-contained chunks that embed more faithfully and retrieve more reliably.

The bimodal quality distribution highlights that retrieval — not generation — is the primary bottleneck. When relevant context is retrieved, GPT produces accurate answers; when it is not, the system fails regardless of the splitter used.

Limitations. Our experiment uses a single corpus, a single embedding model, a single chunk size, and English-language questions only. The GPT-as-judge evaluation, while scalable, introduces subjectivity that human evaluation could mitigate. The relatively small test set (195 questions) limits statistical power for fine-grained comparisons.

Safety Evaluation of Generative Driving Videos

Posted on 2026-01-01 Edited on 2026-07-19 In Archive

Introduction

In recent years generative driving video models developed quickly. It can generate driving scenes and it is very useful for autonomous driving, because it can be used in simulation, testing, data generation and so on. Generating real driving data often cost more money, more time consuming and sometimes it is hard to collect some dangerous or unusual scenes, so generative driving video models become more and more essential in real life. Safety and reliability are very important in autonomous driving because temporary inconsistent, semantic and logical errors can mislead later testing and interpretation. This project aims to use the provided video dataset and emphasize on analyzing the safety issues and behaviours in the generated videos. Through identify, categorize and evaluate this problems, we study whether these generated videos are reliable. Our group project has 3 main parts: define and identify unsafe cases, evaluate them with human and automated methods.

Literature Review

Generative video models have recently become an important research direction, and autonomous driving is one of their most promising application areas. GAIA-1 is a representative example of this line of work. It formulates autonomous driving as a generative world modeling problem and uses video, text, and action inputs to generate realistic driving scenarios with detailed control over ego-vehicle behavior and scene features (Hu et al., 2023). This shows that generative world models can support future prediction, simulation, and simulated data generation for autonomous driving, rather than only producing visually realistic videos. However, GAIA-1 mainly focuses on generation quality, controllability, and future scene modeling, and does not directly address detailed safety evaluation of generated driving videos.

As driving world models continue to develop, evaluation has become increasingly important. DrivingGen argues that the field still lacks a well-designed benchmark for generative driving world models (Zhou et al., 2026). Existing evaluations are often incomplete because generic video metrics cannot fully capture the requirements of driving scenarios. In particular, they often fail to measure safety-critical imaging factors, trajectory plausibility, temporal consistency, agent-level consistency, and controllability under ego conditioning. In order to solve this problem, DrivingGen proposes a benchmark that evaluates generated driving videos from both visual and robotics perspectives, it covers whether it aligns with the real scenario, whether it is continuous, if the paths are generated correctly, and consistency in whether it works as expected. Its results also reveal a clear limitation of current approaches: some models produce visually appealing videos but fail to preserve physical consistency, while others has better performance in following how the objects move but generate weaker image quality. This suggests that generated driving videos cannot be judged only by general visual realism metrics. It also highlights agent-level consistency, especially abnormal disappearance of surrounding agents, which is critical for realism and safety in driving videos.

A more safety-focused perspective is provided by Are AI-Generated Driving Videos Ready for Autonomous Driving? A Diagnostic Evaluation Framework. This paper directly discussed whether AI-generated driving videos can be used in autonomous driving as a reliable source (Xiang et al., 2025). It discovered some typical mistakes in AI-generated driving videos such as physically implausible motion, traffic semantic violations and visual artifacts. It categorized these problems systematically into 2 dimensions: spatiotemporal realism and semantic and behavioral realism. And most importantly, the paper shows that these problems are not only visual flaws, they can also harm later tasks such as detecting the objects, tracking the objects, and separating each object. The authors find that blindly using raw AI-generated driving videos may reduce model performance, while filtering them with a driving-aware evaluator improves results. This suggests that although AI-generated driving videos can work at a larger scale and cost less, they require proper quality control before they can be safely used in autonomous driving pipelines.

Recent work on LLM-based evaluation methods provides a more direct foundation for automated assessment. LLMs-as-Judges: A Comprehensive Survey on LLM-based Evaluation Methods summarizes the idea of using large language models as evaluators to judge the quality, relevance, and effectiveness of generated outputs based on defined criteria (Li et al., 2024). The survey shows that LLM judges are flexible, can work on large scale, and are able to provide natural language explanations. These are attractive traits compared with costly human evaluation or fixed automatic metrics. It also reviews several method settings, such as single-LLM, multi-LLM, and human-AI collaborative evaluation, as well as pointwise, pairwise, and listwise judging (Li et al., 2024). In the meanwhile, this work points out LLM-based evaluation can still be sensitive to prompt, and it might have differences, even possible to have hallucination, or lack sufficient knowledge in a professional field. These limitations suggest that although LLM-based evaluation is promising for generated driving videos, it should still be used carefully and compared with human judgments.

The existing papers have pushed forward this progress from different angles, some focus on generative driving models, some focus on driving-specific benchmarks, some studied failure modes and safety risks, others discussed LLM-based evaluation methods. Although these are all valuable work, they still can’t fix the problem in consistency evaluation and solve generated driving videos’ safety issue. It still lacks a more driving-aware evaluation framework and a more systematic way of identifying unsafe behaviors. This gap motivates our project to discover a clearer way to identify error types and compare the automated evaluation and human judgments in order to improve the evaluation framework.

Dataset and Problem Setting

The dataset used in this project consists of 100 multi-view driving video clips, derived from a nuScenes-style autonomous driving scenario collection. Each clip is 2 seconds long, sampled at 4 frames per second, yielding 8 frames per video. Each frame is arranged as three panels: the top panel shows the real ground-truth footage from six surround-view cameras (front-left, front, front-right, back-right, back, back-left), the middle panel displays a 3D bounding-box overlay, and the bottom panel shows the model-generated footage for the same six views.

Following the project brief, the dataset contains both clean samples and unsafe samples, with the expectation that roughly 30% of clips exhibit semantic errors, logical inconsistencies, or unsafe driving decisions. After human annotation, the realized distribution of our dataset was 40 safe samples and 60 samples with at least one safety-relevant issue, giving an unsafe rate of 60%. This higher rate likely reflects the richness of the fine-grained taxonomy we used.

The evaluation task is to identify whether the generated footage in each clip contains safety-relevant defects compared to the ground-truth reference, and to characterize those defects along three levels of analysis specified by the project brief:

  1. Semantic-level issues: concern the correctness of traffic-relevant elements within a single frame. Examples include missing or hallucinated vehicles and pedestrians, corrupted or illegible road signs, distorted lane markings, and objects that switch types between rendered instances.

  2. Logical-level issues: concern physical and temporal consistency across the 8 frames. Examples include rigid bodies that warp or melt as the camera passes, objects that flicker or texture-boil, and shadows or reflections that fail to track their source.

  3. Decision-level issues: concern the behavior implied by the generated footage for the ego vehicle or surrounding agents. Examples include ego motion that does not match the ground-truth motion, agents making physically impossible maneuvers, and collision-risk geometries.

The goal of the project is to develop evaluation criteria, conduct both human and automated evaluation on the 100 clips, and compare the two methods to characterize the reliability of LLM-based safety assessment.

Methodology

Our evaluation framework has three components: a fine-grained taxonomy of safety issues, a continuous scoring scheme that supports graded severity rather than binary labels, and a two-stage evaluation procedure combining human annotation with LLM-based automated evaluation.

Scoring scheme

Building on the three-level structure from the project brief, we refined each level into a set of concrete sub-categories. The final taxonomy contains 18 sub-categories: six semantic (e.g. object_disappearance, signage_corruption), six logical (e.g. rigid_body_warping, temporal_flicker, occlusion_failure), and six decision (e.g. boundary_breach, collision_risk, motion_mismatch). Fine-grained sub-categories make individual failure modes identifiable during analysis and reduce annotator ambiguity compared to a flat three-class scheme.

Each individual issue receives a score on a discrete 0.0 to 1.0 scale, where 0.0 denotes no issue, 0.2 very minor, 0.4 moderate, 0.6 clearly unsafe, 0.8 severe, and 1.0 critical. For each video, we compute three category scores: semantic_score, logical_score, decision_score. The maximum issue score within that category is the overall score, and a final_score as the mean of the three category scores. Using the maximum within a category prevents a single severe issue from being diluted by smaller issues in the same level, while averaging across levels keeps the final score sensitive to whether safety problems are concentrated or spread across multiple aspects of the scene.

Human evaluation

We jointly annotated the 100 clips. A 20-clip calibration round was performed first: we annotated the same 20 videos independently and reconciled disagreements to align interpretations of the sub-categories and the severity scale. The remaining 80 videos were then divided among the team. All annotations were consolidated into a single JSON dataset using a conversion script that computes semantic_score, logical_score, decision_score, and final_score from the spreadsheet.

Automated evaluation

For automated evaluation we used Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite, which accepts short videos natively without frame extraction. Each video was passed to the model with a single structured system prompt: (a) explained the three-panel layout and instructed the model to evaluate only the bottom row, (b) defined the 18 sub-categories, © reproduced the 0.0–1.0 scoring scale, and (d) included several few-shot calibration examples spanning fully safe, single-issue, mixed, and severe multi-category cases. The model was instructed to return only a valid JSON object containing the three category scores, the final score, a list of structured issue entries, and a short natural-language reason.

Prompt design followed two iterations. The initial prompt produced a strong safe bias: 94 of 100 videos scored 0.0, missing most human-identified issues. We attributed this to the framing of our few-shot examples, which implicitly suggested a conservative default. We revised the prompt to adopt a stricter mindset: the opening now states that generative models frequently produce artifacts and that the job of evaluator is to find issues rather than grant benefit of the doubt. We added a “FLAG these and DO NOT FLAG these” section listing concrete minimum scores for common issue types (e.g. a missing pedestrian scores ≥ 0.4, a warped vehicle ≥ 0.6), replaced a fully safe example with one containing a minor logical issue, and warned against defaulting to the safe pattern. This revised prompt was used for the final evaluation run.

Reproducibility

The full pipeline, including Excel-to-JSON conversion, prompt templates, video upload, Gemini API calls with retry logic for transient 429/500/503 errors, and persistent progress saving, is implemented as a Colab notebook. The resulting dataset file contains every video’s human labels, Gemini scores, raw model output, and structured issue list, enabling the comparative analysis presented in the following section.

Limitation of Prompt-Based Evaluation

While prompt-based LLM evaluation provides a scalable and transparent way to grade model performance, our analysis across three model runs shows three critical bottlenecks:

a. Temporal Blind Spot. The models find it difficult to detect logical errors from static frames containing no 3D spatial data. Thus, the models are unable to perceive motion discontinuities and geometric inconsistencies in the video leading to safety concerns.

b. Prompting Sensitivity. We observe a jump from 68% to 70% using the same model (Flash-lite) with a different prompt. Therefore, model accuracy depends on prompt phrasing rather than scene understanding for reasoning and outcome.

c. Failure in Counterfactual Reasoning. By relying on frame-by-frame visuals, the current VLMs lack reasoning to evaluate intentions or behavior, thus resulting in the near-zero correlation (r ≈ 0) observed in decision error categories.

Common Failure Patterns in Generated Driving Video Model

Analysis of model outputs identifies three recurring failures that undermine the safety of generated driving environments:

a. Object Persistence Failures. The most frequent issue is the inability to maintain a consistent object registry, this shows as object disappearances (29 instances) or hallucinations (3 instances). The models frequently vanish existing objects or generates new ones, especially affecting small entities such as distant pedestrians and cyclists.

b. Temporal Incoherence. Because of a lack of temporal memory, videos exhibit rigid body warping (12 instances) and temporal flicker (15 instances), causing “boiling” textures and morphing silhouettes. In particular, the V3 evaluator demonstrated a higher sensitivity to these changes than humans, showing that automated tools can excel in detecting subtle stability errors.

c. Semantic Label Drift. This category involves category mutation or environmental cue corruption, including class mutation (12 instances) and signage corruption (10 instances). The latter presents extreme risk, as models may generate plausible but semantically incorrect content, such as a green light when a stop is required.

Conclusion

Key Findings

This project designed and applied a systematic safety evaluation framework for generative autonomous driving video models, operating on a 100-sample dataset with human annotated labels as ground truth. Three evaluation runs using two variants of the Gemini 2.5 model family revealed a consistent pattern that automated prompt-based evaluators capture the presence and rough magnitude of semantic errors reasonably well, but substantially underperform compare to humans in detecting logical and decision-level failures. The best automated agreement with human labels was 70% (V2, Gemini Flash-Lite), while the strongest model (V3, Gemini Flash) achieved only 61% agreement despite producing the richest per-issue descriptions as a consequence of inflated false positive rates.

The human label analysis established that semantic errors account for more than 60% of all safety issues in generative driving videos, with object disappearance being the single most frequent failure mode. Decision-level errors are rare in the current dataset but disproportionately high in safety impact, warranting a dedicated evaluation strategies.

Safety Risk of Generative Driving Models

“Silent data corruption” poses a critical risk in that if generative models are used for training, errors in lane markings or signage may be ingested as ground truth, causing catastrophic downstream learning failures. Multi-view inconsistency are bad for camera based systems as video that looks to be locally plausible but is geometrically inconsistent across views can corrupt 3D perception modules, thus leading to faulty depth and localization estimates. Additionally, current VLMs are not able to detect decision-level errors indicating that they cannot serve as safety enforcers yet. Consequently, human or physics-based oversight is essential to certify that generated scenarios exhibit realistic, legally compliant driving decisions.

Directions for Improvement

To enhance the robustness of evaluations and the safety of generative models, the following aspects can be examined:

  • Hybrid Evaluation Pipelines: Physics based systems can be combined with and act as logic checkers for VLM models so as to verify real world constraints.
  • Refined Assessment Logic: Chain-of-thought prompting and structured rubrics with calibrated severity metrics can be used to align automated score with human annotation. Also, fine-tuning lightweight VLMs on annotated safety data may further improve correlation.
  • Model Robustness: Can implement temporal memory or attention and explicit consistency losses during generation to enforce object persistence and multi-view geometry. Additionally, adversarial data augmentation could be used to mitigate issues such as signage corruption.

Confidence-Aware ProSST for Zero-Shot Mutation Effect Prediction on ProteinGym

Posted on 2026-01-01 Edited on 2026-07-19 In Archive

Abstract

ProSST (NeurIPS 2024) improves protein mutation effect prediction by adding a quantized structural channel to a protein language model, but it treats all structural tokens as equally reliable. This project asks whether residue-level AlphaFold pLDDT can improve zero-shot scoring on ProteinGym by modulating that channel at inference time or at score level. We study three embedding-side variants: hard masking below a pLDDT threshold, soft scaling by pLDDT/100, and a learned sigmoid gate trained with a frozen-backbone masked-language-model objective. On 217 ProteinGym assays, our ProSST-2048 reproduction reaches mean Spearman 0.5225 (95% CI [0.500, 0.545]), slightly above the paper’s reported 0.504. None of the direct confidence-aware variants improves over this baseline: hard masking at τ = 50/70/90 yields 0.5203 / 0.5177 / 0.4832, soft weighting yields 0.5203, and learned gating yields 0.5218. We then evaluate score-level confidence conditioning on the 214-assay intersection where ProSST-128, ProSST-512, ProSST-2048, and ESM-2 scores are all available. A flat stacked ensemble reaches 0.5569, and a confidence-conditioned gated ensemble further improves this to 0.5631 (95% CI [0.542, 0.585]). The largest gain appears in the lowest-confidence quartile, which rises from 0.4918 to 0.5138. These results suggest a sharper conclusion: simple residue-wise confidence modulation does not improve ProSST at the embedding stage, but confidence is still useful when it controls how different predictors are combined at the assay level.

1. Introduction

1.1 Problem and Motivation

Structure-aware protein language models often outperform sequence-only models for mutation effect prediction because they can condition on local geometry in addition to sequence context. ProSST is a recent example: it discretizes local structure into tokens and injects them through disentangled sequence–structure attention. The practical weakness of this setup is that predicted structures are not uniformly reliable. AlphaFold’s pLDDT varies considerably across residues and proteins, especially in flexible or poorly constrained regions.

This motivates a natural question: if structural confidence is already available, can we use it to improve ProSST’s zero-shot mutation scores? The idea is attractive because it promises a lightweight improvement without retraining the full backbone. It is also scientifically useful even if it fails: if pLDDT-aware modulation does not help, that tells us something non-trivial about how ProSST already encodes structure reliability.

1.2 Why The Problem Is Difficult

The challenge is not just to add confidence, but to add it at the right level. pLDDT is a coarse scalar confidence proxy, while ProSST’s structure vocabulary and attention machinery are already learned representations of local geometry. An overly simple rule may overwrite useful signal rather than refine it. Negative results are also easy to misread unless the baseline is reproduced on the same assay set and statistical uncertainty is reported.

1.3 Contributions

  • We implement three confidence-aware ProSST variants that modulate the shared structure embedding tensor before the encoder.
  • We reproduce ProSST-2048 on 217 ProteinGym assays with bootstrap confidence intervals and pLDDT-quartile analysis.
  • We show that all three direct pLDDT-aware variants fail to improve zero-shot performance, with aggressive masking causing large regressions.
  • We introduce a confidence-conditioned score-level ensemble that uses pLDDT to gate the relative contribution of multiple predictors and improves over a flat stacked ensemble on the 214-assay overlap.
  • We document an evaluation protocol that treats sequence–confidence alignment as a hard requirement, preventing silent distortion of confidence-aware inputs.

2. Related Work / Background

2.1 Protein Language Models

Sequence-only protein language models such as ESM-1b and ESM-2 learn from large protein corpora with masked-language modeling. Structure-aware models extend this by injecting predicted or derived structural information. SaProt couples each amino acid with a Foldseek 3Di token. ProSST instead learns a discrete structure vocabulary through vector quantization and uses disentangled attention between sequence and structure streams [1]. These approaches typically assume that the structure channel is always trustworthy.

2.2 Structural Confidence

AlphaFold’s pLDDT is the standard per-residue confidence estimate for predicted structures [3, 4]. It is commonly used for filtering low-confidence regions, but it is less clear whether it can be profitably fused into a pretrained structure-aware language model at inference time. This project tests exactly that narrow but meaningful question.

3. Approach

A key architectural detail is that the structure embedding tensor is created once and then reused by every encoder layer through disentangled sequence–structure attention. This makes the embedding stage a natural intervention point: one transformation affects all layers while preserving the original backbone.

  • The model is implemented as a lightweight extension of the public ProSST masked-language model, so the pretrained backbone can be reused directly while only the confidence-aware components are newly added.

4. Experiments

4.1 Data

We evaluate on the 2024 ProteinGym substitution split: 217 assays and roughly 1.5M mutations [2]. Each assay includes a wild-type residue FASTA, a ProSST structure-token FASTA, and a substitutions CSV with experimental DMS scores. pLDDT arrays are extracted from AlphaFoldDB PDB files and aligned to the residue FASTA.

The evaluation protocol enforces exact residue-level alignment between the wild-type sequence and the pLDDT array used for confidence-aware scoring. Any sequence/PDB inconsistency is treated as a data-quality issue that must be resolved before evaluation. This design choice is important for the present study because even small alignment shifts would change which residues are masked or down-weighted, directly confounding the interpretation of the confidence-aware ablations.

For the ensemble analysis, we additionally require that all five score channels be present for the same assay. This yields a 214-assay intersection. The ensemble numbers are therefore reported separately from the 217-assay direct modulation results rather than mixed into a single table.

4.2 Evaluation Metrics

The primary metric is per-assay Spearman’s ρ, averaged across assays. We also report NDCG and top-10% recall. Statistical uncertainty is estimated by bootstrap over assays with n=1000n = 1000n=1000 resamples. To test whether confidence-aware methods help specifically on uncertain proteins, we also stratify assays into four quartiles by mean pLDDT.

4.3 Experimental Settings

All reported CA-ProSST runs use the public ProSST-2048 model with the backbone frozen. Full 217-assay evaluation takes about 40 minutes per variant with the vectorized scorer on H100 hardware. V3 model selection is based on validation loss.

5. Results

Two points stand out in Table 1. First, the baseline reproduction is solid: 0.5225 is slightly above the published ProSST score of 0.504 and its confidence interval comfortably covers that paper number. Second, none of the confidence-aware variants beats baseline. The learned gate is closest, but the difference is only −0.0007 in mean Spearman and is well inside the bootstrap uncertainty range. Hard masking gets worse as the threshold becomes more aggressive, with τ = 90 causing a large drop of −0.0393.

The quartile breakdown in Table 2 confirms that confidence matters at the dataset level: low-confidence proteins are substantially harder than high-confidence proteins under every model. However, direct confidence modulation does not close that gap. The V3 gate is nearly identical to baseline in all quartiles, while the hard-mask variants become progressively worse as more structure is removed.

Table 3 changes the interpretation of the project. The direct embedding-side variants remain negative, but confidence is not useless overall. When it is used to modulate the mixture of heterogeneous predictors rather than to suppress structure embeddings residue by residue, it produces a measurable gain. The flat ensemble already improves strongly over any single channel, reaching 0.5569 mean Spearman on the 214-assay overlap. The oracle comparison shows the same pattern: flat oracle Spearman is 0.5574, while gated oracle Spearman is 0.5639.

The confidence-stratified behavior of the gated ensemble is also informative. Relative to the flat ensemble, the largest gain occurs in the lowest-confidence quartile (0.4918 → 0.5138, +0.0219), while the middle and upper quartiles change only modestly. This is exactly the regime where naive hard masking caused the greatest damage. Confidence therefore appears useful not as a binary filter on structure tokens, but as a signal for how aggressively to trust structurally grounded versus complementary predictors.

6. Analysis

6.1 Per-Assay Gain/Loss Distribution

The learned gate is best understood as a near-baseline model rather than an improvement. Compared with baseline, it improves 83 of the 217 assays and degrades 134. The mean gain on improved assays is only +0.0028 Spearman, while the mean loss on degraded assays is −0.0029. The closeness of these two numbers explains why the aggregate score remains essentially unchanged: the gate learns a weak modulation that neither consistently rescues low-confidence proteins nor consistently harms high-confidence ones.

6.2 What The Ensemble Learns From Confidence

The score-level ensemble behaves differently from the embedding-side gate. On the 214-assay overlap, the gated ensemble improves 139 assays and degrades 75 relative to the flat ensemble, with mean per-assay gain +0.0062. The best gains are concentrated in difficult, lower-confidence assays, while the largest losses are smaller in aggregate and are not confined to a single pLDDT regime. This asymmetric gain/loss pattern explains why the overall mean rises even though the quartile averages outside Q1 move only slightly.

The fitted gating parameters are also interpretable. Across folds, the dominant weight remains on the ProSST-2048 structure channel, but the learned operating points for most structural channels concentrate near mean pLDDT 55 with a relatively smooth temperature of 20. This indicates that the model does not prefer abrupt switching; instead it performs gradual confidence-aware rebalancing. The sequence-only ProSST channel is consistently driven to zero weight, while ESM retains a non-zero contribution and occasionally receives a higher operating point. Empirically, the gated ensemble is therefore not discovering a new backbone-level representation. It is discovering a more effective allocation rule for when to emphasize the strongest structural channel and when to preserve support from a non-structural model.

6.3 Threshold Sensitivity By Confidence Regime

The hard-mask ablation provides a clearer diagnostic. Raising the threshold from 50 to 70 to 90 produces a monotonic decline in overall mean Spearman (0.5203 → 0.5177 → 0.4832), but the damage is highly concentrated in lower-confidence proteins. Under the most aggressive setting, Q1 falls from 0.4490 to 0.3645 and Q2 falls from 0.4982 to 0.4370, whereas Q4 is almost unchanged (0.5744 to 0.5742). This pattern directly contradicts the naive rule that low pLDDT residues should simply be discarded. Even when the predicted structure is imperfect, ProSST still extracts useful signal from it.

6.4 Why The Learned Gate Still Fails

The learned gate also clarifies why additional flexibility is not sufficient by itself. Training loss decreases from 2.2794 to 2.1872 before rising again, and the best validation loss is obtained at epoch 1 (2.0936), followed by a slight regression at epoch 2 (2.1635). This indicates that the gate can fit the auxiliary masked-language-model objective, but only weakly. More importantly, that objective does not directly supervise mutation-effect ranking. Because the backbone is frozen, the gate can only rescale an already-trained structural representation; it cannot relearn how structural confidence should interact with fitness prediction. The empirical result is therefore conservative behavior: the gate remains very close to the pretrained baseline and produces no reliable improvement.

7. Conclusion

This project asked a focused question: can AlphaFold confidence improve zero-shot mutation effect prediction in a structure-aware protein language model? The answer depends on where confidence is injected. On the committed 217-assay ProteinGym benchmark, direct embedding-side modulation does not help: hard masking and soft scaling are consistently worse than baseline, and the learned per-residue gate matches baseline within noise rather than surpassing it. This rules out the simplest intuition that low-confidence structure tokens should merely be attenuated or removed before entering ProSST.

At the same time, the ensemble results show that confidence is not irrelevant. On the 214-assay intersection where multiple predictors are available, confidence-conditioned score-level gating improves over a strong flat ensemble and yields the best overall result of this study. The strongest gain appears in the lowest-confidence quartile, suggesting that pLDDT is most useful when it controls cross-model trust rather than within-model suppression.

Taken together, these findings sharpen the design lesson for confidence-aware protein modeling. Confidence should not be treated as a residue-local mask applied uniformly to pretrained structure features. It is better viewed as a model-selection or mixture-weighting signal that mediates between complementary predictors with different failure modes. Future work should therefore focus on confidence-aware routing, richer uncertainty representations, and training objectives aligned directly with variant-ranking performance.

Appendix A. Reproducibility Details

A.1 Experimental Protocol

Reproducing the reported numbers requires six stages. First, obtain the wild-type sequences, structural token sequences, and substitution tables from the upstream ProSST release. Second, obtain AlphaFoldDB structures and extract residue-aligned pLDDT arrays. Third, evaluate the ProSST-2048 baseline on all 217 assays. Fourth, evaluate the hard-mask and soft-weighting ablations using the same assay set and the same confidence arrays. Fifth, train the learned gate on a held-out protein set that does not overlap the ProteinGym assay targets, select the best validation model, and evaluate that model on the same 217 assays. Sixth, cache the per-mutant scores needed for score-level combination on the 214-assay overlap, fit both the flat and the confidence-conditioned ensembles by cross-validation, and report their assay-level summaries separately from the 217-assay direct-modulation runs.

A.2 Alignment Safeguard

Because the project studies residue-level confidence modulation, exact alignment between the confidence array and the residue sequence is essential. The release therefore treats sequence/PDB inconsistencies as explicit preprocessing errors. This choice prevents a common but under-discussed failure mode in confidence-aware pipelines: a shifted confidence array may still allow the program to run, yet the resulting ablation no longer corresponds to the intended biological positions.
